## Day 1: Scaling Mount Lycabettus
### Landing in Athens, the "hotel with a view"
After a nearly 9 hour plane ride from NYC, Jeff and I landed in Athens. We took a rail from the airport into the city, and stepped outside the Syntagma subway station onto a bright and balmy day.

It was a 10 minute walk to our hotel, Electra Palace Athens, named for its stunning views of the Acropolis.

Our room didn't necessarily reflect the hotel's namesake, but it was a pleasant space regardless.

<PostImage src={img1} size="xl" caption="The view from our hotel room" captionOverlay={true}/>

After dropping off our luggage and freshening up, we set off to explore the city. Today, we would be heading toward Mount Lycabettus, walking through Monastiraki and the Athens National Garden along the way.

<PostImage src={img2} alt="A distant goalpost: Mount Lycabettus" size="xl" caption="A distant goalpost: Mount Lycabettus" captionOverlay={false}/>

### Monastiraki: Grounding ourselves
#### Souvlaki Kostas $\iff$ Granddaddy Halal
Starving by this point, we first walked to Souvlaki Kostas for a quick bite. Supposedly some of the best Souvlaki in Athens, we queued up behind a line comprised of people speaking Greek, English, French, Chinese—the place attracted tourists and locals alike.

<PostImage src={img3} alt="The ethnically diverse queue outside Souvlaki Kostas" size="xl" caption="The ethnically diverse queue outside Souvlaki Kostas" captionOverlay={true}/>

The interior was very quaint, with pastel teal walls and a retro-American diner feel. Two gruff Greek men sat behind the counter, seemingly friends-of / workers-at the establishment.

<PostImage src={img4} alt="Inside Souvlaki Kostas" size="xl" caption="Inside of Souvlaki Kostas" captionOverlay={true}/>

The souvlaki was good! (And also 4€.) Spiced and grilled pork and beef, served in a warmed pita with lettuce, onions, tomatoes, and tzatziki. It took me back to my late night Halal cart runs at Columbia, and I wondered if this was the flavor profile my lamb over rice order was based off.

#### Our quest for dessert
We finished our souvlaki and continued walking through Monastiraki, a neighborhood filled with flea markets and souvenir stores.

The streets were lined with fruiting orange trees, giving the neighborhood a sweet smell compared to New York's sickly marijuana-sewage feel. I tried grabbing at a couple oranges, but it seemed all of the low-hanging fruits had already been picked.

<PostImage src={img5} alt="Oranges, cafes, and boutiques in Monastiraki" size="xl" caption="Oranges, cafes, and boutiques in Monastiraki" captionOverlay={true}/>

Not letting my height hinder our quest for dessert, we got ice cream from two different street stalls:
- [Goat It Ice Cream Store](https://maps.app.goo.gl/ZMjTt2mXzNQc6JRr6) - Okay, but the vanilla tasted indistinguishable from cow's milk ice cream.
- [La Greche](https://maps.app.goo.gl/YfRkVwzGc4PiGobp6) - Mindblowing pistachio gelato, and my favorite ice cream I've ever eaten.

#### Souvenir shopping, Athens's age

Bellies full, we browsed some of the many souvenir shops in Monastiraki.

We saw a lot of Greek gods—on shirts, keychains, magnets, but also on little statuettes, busts, soap bars. I felt slightly jealous; even Athens's cheap gift shops felt classy, having such a deep mythos to pull from. It made the youth of New York as a city, with its apple and taxicab souvenirs, very apparent in contrast.

<PostImage src={img6} alt="Souvenir shops in Monastiraki" size="xl" caption="Souvenir shops in Monastiraki" captionOverlay={true}/>

That said, the shops also sold a lot more explicitly adult-rated trinkets than those in New York, many of which were even displayed in the windows. There were magnets of scantily clad and naked women (with squish in the right places), phallic keychains, and bells with "ring for sex" engraved on it. Looking back, I wish I bought the sex bell.

## Day 2: The Acropolis
The next morning, we got up in time for breakfast at the hotel. The common dining room was on the top floor of the building, and delivered the hotel's promised panoramic views of the Acropolis—where we were headed today.

<PostImage src={img20} size="xl" caption="The Acropolis in the distance" captionOverlay={true}/>

### Cats and music on the wrong side of the hill

Since the Acropolis was visible from a distance, we decided to navigate towards it without Google Maps.

Upon reaching a fork near the base, we headed in the direction opposite the Acropolis entrance.

This side seemed residential, with enclaves of shaded plaster homes and potted gardens. 

Passing by more cats, I approached to pet one and noticed behind her was a 60-something year old man strumming his guitar.

The man asks where we're from. Jeff replies, "New York," and the man goes, "Oh New York! Frank Sinatra!" Jeff laughs and nods, and the man starts singing a song about us in Greek. The only words I made out among the Greek were "Jeffrey," "Sarah," "New York," and "Frank Sinatra."

When we got back to the right side of the hill, the path to the Acropolis became clear. We could see crowds of families with large backpacks and sunglasses, across a wider diversity of races and ethnicities. 

### The Acropolis

#### Is huge!
The Parthenon stood impressively at the top, surrounded by ruins of other buildings. In my Art Humanities class, we spent an entire unit dissecting the Parthenon, the many layers of enscribed pictographs on its walls, its tiered chambers, and the significance of overlooking the entire city and in-turn being visible from anywhere. 

Seeing it in person, the only thing I could think about was how large it was.

Feeling peckish, we held off on looking through the Acropolis Museum until after lunch. 

Instead we walked back down toward Plaka, the old historical neighborhood of Athens found a little east of the Acropolis. Its narrow, stepped streets felt like they were made for foot traffic, save the occasional delivery moped.

#### Two very indulgent dinners near Xayteia
After mulling about Plaka and Monastiraki for a while, we walked to an early dinner at [Tavern Klimataria](https://klimataria.gr/). With 4.4 stars and 9,620 Google reviews, we had high hopes. The meal itself was somewhat disappointing, with some tough cuts of lamb and stale bread. Jeff says the highlight of this meal was the bowl of baked cheese with sausage floating in it.

Looking to redeem the meal, we pinned our last stop of the day on [Stani](https://www.stani1931.com/), a dairy shop in Xayteia.

We passed by Omonoia Square en-route. More modernized than the other parts of Athens, we saw a giant central fountain surrounded by 4-lane wide automobile roads and blocky 5+ story buildings. It felt eerily sparse.

Walking through here, made me think how far Athens was past its prime. The square itself felt like the commercialized and artificial plazas of Downtown Brooklyn, and beyond its radius the buildings looked worn and rusted over.

<PostImage src={img46} size="xl" caption="A butchery in Xayteia" captionOverlay={true}/>

Atmosphere aside, [Stani](https://www.stani1931.com/) itself was a highlight of the trip. 
We feasted on traditional Greek desserts—loukoumades, galaktoboureko, sheeps-milk yogurt with honey and walnuts, and their take on a mille feuille. Jeff even got a cup of milk.

## Day 3: Pitstop in Exarcheia
### Antikythera mechanism
Our flight was in the afternoon, so we took the morning to look around the National Archaeological Museum. Jeff put it on the itinerary so he could check out the Antikythera mechanism, known as the "world's first computer." 

<PostImage src={img45_light} darkSrc={img45_dark} size="md" caption="A diagrammatic Antikythera" captionOverlay={true}/>

It was a device made of several interlocking gears, allowing you to input an arbitrary date and compute the positions of the sun, solar system, and moon for that date. We sat in front of its ruins trying to figure out how it worked, remarked on how smart these people were, and left.

### Last bites in Exarcheia
We walked around the neighboring graffiti-painted streets of Exarcheia afterward. The residents seemed standoffish compared to the tourist-hungry shopkeepers of Plaka and Monastiraki. (Still, the place had more soul than Xayteia.) Jeff described it in post as "what Bushwick wants to be."

<PostImage src={exarcheia} size="xl" caption="Street art in Exarcheia ([Athens Insider](https://www.athensinsider.com/an-exarcheia-resident-shares-her-insider-secrets/))" captionOverlay={true}/>

After another quick souvlaki from [Giorgos Manos](https://maps.app.goo.gl/oDtZ8WnuryMkr5uCA), we headed back to the hotel, grabbed our bags, and set flight for Paris.
